Unit 1: Locational Significance & Administrative Divisions

Geographical Location

Assam is strategically located in the northeastern region of India, serving as the gateway to the Seven Sister States.

Location: 24°8'N to 28°2'N latitude and 89°42'E to 96°E longitude
  • Total Area: 78,438 sq km (approximately)
  • Position: Bordered by Bhutan and Arunachal Pradesh (North), Nagaland and Manipur (East), Meghalaya, Tripura, Mizoram and Bangladesh (South), West Bengal (West)
  • Rank: Largest state among North-Eastern states in terms of area

Strategic Importance

  • Gateway to Northeast: Acts as the corridor connecting the northeastern states with the rest of India
  • International Borders: Shares borders with Bhutan (international) and Bangladesh, making it geopolitically significant
  • Trade Route: Historical trade routes connecting India with Southeast Asia passed through Assam
  • Defense Strategy: Located in a sensitive border area, crucial for national security
  • Cultural Bridge: Connects Indian mainland culture with Southeast Asian influences

Physiographic Significance

  • Brahmaputra Valley: One of the most fertile river valleys in the world
  • Rich Biodiversity: Located in the Indo-Burma biodiversity hotspot
  • Natural Resources: Abundant forest cover, wildlife, mineral resources (oil, natural gas, coal)

Ancient Period (Pragjyotishpur & Kamarupa Kingdom)

  • Assam was known as Pragjyotishpura in ancient texts
  • The powerful Kamarupa Kingdom (4th-12th century) covered present-day Assam and parts of neighboring states
  • Kingdom was divided into administrative units called Vishayas

Ahom Period (1228-1826)

  • Ahom kings ruled Assam for nearly 600 years
  • Administrative system based on Paik system (organized labor force)
  • Territory divided into Bars (provinces) and Mels (subdivisions)
  • Main divisions: Upper Assam, Central Assam, and Lower Assam

British Colonial Period (1826-1947)

Treaty of Yandabo - 1826 (Assam came under British control)
  • 1826: Assam annexed by British after Treaty of Yandabo (First Anglo-Burmese War)
  • 1874: Assam became a separate Chief Commissioner's Province
  • Initial Division: Divided into Upper Assam and Lower Assam divisions
  • Districts Created: Kamrup, Darrang, Nowgong, Sibsagar, Lakhimpur, Cachar, Goalpara
  • 1905-1911: Temporary partition - Assam merged with East Bengal (reversed in 1911)
  • 1912: Assam again became a separate province with Shillong as capital

Administrative Changes Under British

  • Introduction of Revenue Districts and Sub-divisions
  • Creation of Deputy Commissioner posts for district administration
  • Establishment of Municipal Boards in urban areas
  • Development of tea estates led to special administrative arrangements

Major Territorial Changes

1947: Assam at Independence had 14 districts
  • 1947: Partition of India - Sylhet district (majority Muslim area) went to East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) after referendum
  • 1963: Nagaland carved out of Assam (became 16th state of India)
  • 1972: Meghalaya became a separate state (earlier part of Assam as United Khasi-Jaintia Hills and Garo Hills)
  • 1972: Mizoram and Arunachal Pradesh became Union Territories (separated from Assam)
  • 1987: Arunachal Pradesh and Mizoram attained full statehood

Impact of Reorganization

  • Assam's area reduced from approximately 1,42,000 sq km to 78,438 sq km
  • Loss of hill districts led to focus on plains development
  • Administrative focus shifted to managing ethnic and linguistic diversity
  • Created challenges in resource management and revenue generation

Capital Changes

  • Before 1972: Shillong (now in Meghalaya) was the capital
  • After 1972: Dispur (suburb of Guwahati) became the new capital of Assam

Divisions of Assam

Assam is divided into 5 administrative divisions for better governance:

  1. Upper Assam Division: Dibrugarh, Tinsukia, Sivasagar, Jorhat, Golaghat, Charaideo, Majuli
  2. Lower Assam Division: Bongaigaon, Kokrajhar, Dhubri, Goalpara, Barpeta, Chirang, Baksa, South Salmara
  3. Central Assam Division: Nagaon, Morigaon, Hojai
  4. North Assam Division: Lakhimpur, Dhemaji, Sonitpur, Biswanath, Udalguri, Darrang
  5. Barak Valley Division: Cachar, Karimganj, Hailakandi

Autonomous Councils

For protection of tribal rights and culture, autonomous councils exist:

  • Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC): Covers Kokrajhar, Chirang, Baksa, Udalguri districts
  • Karbi Anglong Autonomous Council
  • Dima Hasao Autonomous Council (North Cachar Hills)
  • Mising Autonomous Council
  • Rabha Hasong Autonomous Council
  • Sonowal Kachari Autonomous Council
  • Thengal Kachari Autonomous Council
  • Tiwa Autonomous Council
  • Deori Autonomous Council

Urban Administrative Units

  • Municipal Corporations: Guwahati
  • Municipal Boards: Dibrugarh, Jorhat, Silchar, Nagaon, Tinsukia, Tezpur, Dhubri (7 cities)
  • Town Committees: 44+ towns

Important Exam Questions (Unit 1)

Q1: Discuss the locational significance of Assam. (5 marks) [file:40]

Answer:

Introduction: Assam is located in the northeastern region of India between 24°8'N to 28°2'N latitude and 89°42'E to 96°E longitude.

Key Points:

  • Gateway Position: Acts as the corridor and gateway to the entire northeastern region, connecting seven sister states
  • Strategic Location: Shares international borders with Bhutan and Bangladesh, making it geopolitically important for India's defense
  • Trade Significance: Historical trade routes to Southeast Asia passed through Assam, making it a commercial hub
  • Biodiversity Hotspot: Located in Indo-Burma biodiversity region with rich flora and fauna
  • Resource Rich: Presence of Brahmaputra valley, oil fields, tea gardens, and forests adds economic significance

Conclusion: Assam's location makes it strategically, economically, and culturally significant for India and the entire Northeast region.

Q2: Write a note on the administrative changes in Assam during Pre and Post-Independence period. (10 marks)

PRE-INDEPENDENCE (British Period 1826-1947):

  • 1826: Assam came under British rule after Treaty of Yandabo
  • 1874: Became a separate Chief Commissioner's Province
  • Divided into districts: Kamrup, Darrang, Nowgong, Sibsagar, Lakhimpur, Cachar, Goalpara
  • 1905-1911: Temporarily merged with East Bengal, then separated again
  • 1912: Shillong became the capital

POST-INDEPENDENCE (After 1947):

  • 1947: Sylhet district went to East Pakistan after referendum
  • 1963: Nagaland carved out and became separate state
  • 1972: Meghalaya became separate state; Mizoram and Arunachal Pradesh became Union Territories
  • 1972: Capital shifted from Shillong to Dispur
  • 1987: Arunachal Pradesh and Mizoram attained full statehood
  • Present: Assam reduced to 78,438 sq km with 35 districts

Impact: Reorganization reduced Assam's area but helped in better administration and gave identity to various ethnic groups.

Q3: Explain the present administrative divisions of Assam. (5 marks)

Current Structure: Assam has 35 districts organized into 5 administrative divisions.

Five Divisions:

  • Upper Assam: 7 districts (Dibrugarh, Tinsukia, Sivasagar, Jorhat, Golaghat, Charaideo, Majuli)
  • Lower Assam: 8 districts (Bongaigaon, Kokrajhar, Dhubri, Goalpara, Barpeta, etc.)
  • Central Assam: 3 districts (Nagaon, Morigaon, Hojai)
  • North Assam: 6 districts (Lakhimpur, Dhemaji, Sonitpur, Biswanath, Udalguri, Darrang)
  • Barak Valley: 3 districts (Cachar, Karimganj, Hailakandi)

Special Features:

  • Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) provides autonomous governance to 4 districts
  • Two hill districts have autonomous councils: Karbi Anglong and Dima Hasao
  • Guwahati is the only Municipal Corporation; Dispur is the state capital

Q4: What is the total land area of Assam? (1 mark) [file:40]

Answer: The total land area of Assam is approximately 78,438 square kilometers (78,438 sq km).

Q5: Write the rank of Assam among North-Eastern States in terms of area. (2 marks) [file:41]

Answer: Assam ranks 1st (First/Largest) among the North-Eastern States in terms of geographical area. With 78,438 sq km, it is the largest state in the northeastern region of India, followed by Arunachal Pradesh.

Q6: Name the country which shares international border with Assam. (1 mark) [file:41]

Answer: The two countries that share international borders with Assam are:

  • Bhutan (Northern border)
  • Bangladesh (Southern and Western border)

Q7: Why is Assam called the land of rivers? Explain. (5 marks) [file:40]

Answer:

Introduction: Assam is often called the "Land of Rivers" due to the presence of numerous rivers and their tributaries flowing through the state.

Reasons:

  • Brahmaputra River System: The mighty Brahmaputra is one of the world's largest rivers, flowing 890 km through Assam
  • Numerous Tributaries: Over 50 tributaries join Brahmaputra including Subansiri, Manas, Dibang, Lohit, Dhansiri, Kopili, etc.
  • Barak River System: Another major river system in southern Assam (Barak Valley)
  • Economic Importance: Rivers provide water for irrigation, fisheries, and transportation
  • Cultural Significance: Rivers are integral to Assamese culture, festivals (like Bihu), and daily life
  • Ecological Role: Support rich biodiversity, wetlands, and fertile alluvial plains

Conclusion: The dense network of rivers has shaped Assam's geography, economy, and culture, justifying the name "Land of Rivers."
